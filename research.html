The Challenge: "One-Size-Fits-All" Fails

Online higher education faces persistently high failure and dropout rates. Current Early Warning Systems (EWS) often rely on static demographic thresholds, ignoring crucial individual Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) behaviors like pacing, engagement, and consistency.

This interactive application outlines a research methodology to develop a personalized, AI-driven EWS using the comprehensive OULAD dataset. By analyzing daily Virtual Learning Environment (VLE) clickstreams alongside demographic data, we aim to provide instructors with actionable, explainable insights to intervene before a student fails.

1. The Baseline: Understanding the Learner Profile

To build a predictive model, we first must understand the historical outcomes. This section explores the studentInfo.csv data. While demographics alone are insufficient for an accurate EWS, they form the foundation. Notice the high combined rate of Withdrawals and Failures—highlighting the critical need for intervention.

Overall Student Outcomes

Nearly 53% of students in the dataset either withdrew or failed, underscoring the retention crisis in self-paced online learning.

Outcomes by Prior Education

Students entering with "Lower Than A Level" qualifications show significantly higher withdrawal rates, identifying an initially vulnerable cohort.

2. The Behavioral Engine: VLE Clickstreams

Building on findings by Hobbs (2016), static demographics are augmented with dynamic behavioral data (studentVle.csv). Interaction volume—specifically in forums and active content modules—acts as a proxy for Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) strategies. Active engagement heavily correlates with final assessment scores.

🔍 The Hobbs Correlation

Hobbs identified that active participation (posting in forums, early assessment submission) is a stronger predictor of success than mere content viewing. The scatter plot visualizes this: higher interaction volumes cluster toward the "Pass" and "Distinction" ranges.

⚠️ The "Lurker" Risk

Students with low interaction days (bottom left quadrant) are at the highest risk of withdrawal. A temporal model captures this decay in engagement week over week.

3. Proposed Methodology: Temporal Modeling & XAI

An Early Warning System is useless if it predicts failure on the last day of the course. The proposed model uses Temporal Slicing to predict outcomes at critical junctures (e.g., Week 2, Week 4). Furthermore, using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), the model transforms from a "Black Box" into an actionable tool for instructors.
